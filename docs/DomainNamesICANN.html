ICANN



The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN), is an organization in charge of the namespaces and numerical spaces of the Internet. In other words, ICANN maintain and administers the Domain Name System (DNS). This organization is the one that defines the top-level domains and the operation of the root name servers. They also mange the Internet Protocol, assign the address spaces for IPv4 and IPv6, and control the time zone database management.

Prior to ICANN, the Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA) was the one in charge of registering and managing the top level domains and the IP address allocation. Until 1998, when the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) proposed a rule making Green Paper which attempted to privatize the management of internet names and addresses with the purpose of developing competition and facilitate global participation. As a result of this proposal ICANN was formed and it managed the IANA under contract to the U.S. Department of Commerce.

ICANN holds a broad representation the global internet community and follows a community driven policy making to keep pace with the constant changing technology. It manages the security and stability of DNS while enabling new technologies and maintaining interoperability.


Domain Names

In short, a domain name is an identification string. It defines the authority or control of an application, website, or computer within the internet or a network. A domain name follows the rules and procedures of DNS, therefore, are organized in subordinate levels of the DNS root domains.

A domain name can represent a collection of internet resources or an individual resource. The first level of domain names are called top-level domains (TLDs). The registration of any domain name is managed within a domain name registrar.
