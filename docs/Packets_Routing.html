The Internet - Packets, Routing, and Reliability


The internet is formed by multiple independent networks connected to each other. There are also multiple ways of transmitting data within the internet, however, what is the actual process in order to send and receive such data?


Packets


Any type of digital information in the internet is sent via packets. A packet contains the information you would like to send along with the destination IP address and a source IP address. Just like all the data in the internet a packet is formed by bits, however, there might be files that you would like to share that are too big in order to fit in a single packet, therefore, this data is chopped into multiple packets and sent through the network. Whenever the packets arrive at the destination, they are sorted out and put together in order to reconstruct the original data. Information on the internet does not need to follow a fixed path. When a packet is sent, it could take a completely different route to reach its destination every single time.


Routers


Routers are special computers that act like traffic managers and keep packets moving through the internet. A router keeps track of multiple routes for sending packets, when the network is congested routers might change the path that a packet is following in order to ensure it reaches the destination. This decision is made by choosing the cheapest available route for each packet, cheapest meaning the path that will take the shortest time and also considering the known routes.


Reliability


Due to its distributed design, the internet is fault tolerant. Even if one of the paths to transmit data fails then the data can still reach its destination by following a different path. Along with this distributed design, there are also other protocols that ensure the reliability of the data being sent and received, like the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP). TCP keeps track of all the data being sent through the network, once it reaches the destination TCP sends an acknowledgement flag to the sender confirming that all packets have been received.
